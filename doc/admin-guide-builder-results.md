# Admin Guide: Reading Builder Results

How to interpret the Builder Score, the trait signals, and the written answers to make
hiring decisions.

---

## Quick Start

1. Open `/admin?token=dev-builder-admin-2024` → **Startup Builders** tab.
2. **Scan view:** sort by Builder % or money flag to find strong candidates and red flags.
3. **Click any row to expand** and see the full picture: trait breakdown, written answers,
   and what drives them.
4. **Read the written answers yourself** — they're the final word on judgment, depth, and fit.

---

## Understanding the Score

**Builder Score** (0–100) is a weighted blend of 7 traits. It's a **sorting and flagging aid**,
not a verdict. Use it to rank, but decide based on the full picture.

- **80–100:** Strong builder signals across the board. Still read the written answers to
  confirm depth.
- **60–79:** Solid on most traits, weak or inconsistent on one or two. Look at which traits
  are lower and why (written answers will hint).
- **40–59:** Mixed signals. May be strong in one area (e.g., learning) but weak in another
  (e.g., ownership). Worth a paid trial if the writing shows real depth.
- **0–39:** Major red flags or inconsistency. Likely doesn't match the builder profile.
  Review the money flag and the written answers before passing.

---

## The 7 Traits Explained

### 1. Drive & Motivation (0–100)
**What it reads:** Does the person keep going on hard, unglamorous work without being chased?

**How it's scored:**
- Scenario s8: boring, invisible task that matters (perseverance test)
- Written answer: "worked hard with no money" (real intrinsic motivation)
- Vision/direction depth (long-term pull, not just weekly tasks)

**What to look for in the written answer:**
- Specificity (what was the task, why did it matter, what did they actually do?)
- Unglamorous work — not "I shipped a cool feature," but "I debugged a data pipeline no one cares about."
- Why they kept going when there was no obvious reward.

**Red flag:** "I did it because my boss told me to" or vague, short answer.

### 2. Growth & Learning (0–100)
**What it reads:** Does the person learn fast and independently? Do they run toward things
they can't do yet?

**How it's scored:**
- Scenario s10: take a stretch project you might fail at publicly (growth-seeking despite ego risk)
- Scenario s12: learn a new tool with no training (independent learning)
- Written answer: learning under fire (STAR behavioral)

**What to look for in the written answer:**
- They took initiative to learn, not waited for a course or training.
- They applied it immediately (urgency signals real learning, not hobby learning).
- They measured success concretely ("I got X result" not "I felt confident").

**Red flag:** "I watched YouTube tutorials" without shipping anything, or "I waited for proper training."

### 3. Ownership & Hard Work (0–100)
**What it reads:** Does the person treat problems as theirs even when "not my job"?
Do they find a way?

**How it's scored:**
- Scenario s1: vague goal, owner offline (take initiative vs wait for clarity)
- Scenario s4: no budget, real deadline (scrappy solutions vs "can't be done")
- Scenario s11: important problem clearly outside your role (ownership beyond title)

**What to look for in the written answer:**
- They solved a problem that *wasn't* formally their responsibility.
- They didn't wait; they found a way with what they had.
- They didn't shift blame ("my team wasn't organized") — they owned the solution.

**Red flag:** "I told my boss and waited for them to assign it" or "It wasn't my job so I
focused on my own work."

### 4. Startup Tolerance (0–100)
**What it reads:** Comfortable with ambiguity, changing priorities, limited resources.
Doesn't crumble under startup chaos.

**How it's scored:**
- Scenario s2: priorities shift, feature gets deprioritised (adaptability)
- Scenario s1: ambiguity and moving forward (tolerance for fog)

**What to look for in the written answer:**
- They don't panic when plans change mid-week.
- They focus on what matters *now*, not sunk costs.
- They see constraint as a problem to solve, not a reason to give up.

**Red flag:** "I hate changing priorities" or answers that assume perfect, stable conditions.

### 5. Low Ego, High Standard (0–100)
**What it reads:** Takes hard feedback. Credits others. Refuses to ship junk.

**How it's scored:**
- Scenario s3: junior flags a real flaw in your work publicly (ego response)
- Scenario s5: quality vs deadline (standard)
- Scenario s10: take a stretch you might fail at (ego strength)

**What to look for in the written answer:**
- They credit others and collaborators.
- When they describe a mistake or flaw, they own it directly ("I did X wrong").
- They talk about raising quality bars, not just hitting targets.

**Red flag:** "Everyone was wrong except me" or "I'd ship it and fix it later" (without
holding a quality bar).

### 6. Long-term Ambition (0–100)
**What it reads:** Finishes what they start. Builds something that lasts. Not distracted
by shiny things.

**How it's scored:**
- Scenario s9: flashy new opportunity vs 12-month commitment (consistency)
- Written answer: vision (3–5 years, why this work matters to them)

**What to look for in the written answer:**
- A concrete vision, not "I want to grow and learn" (everyone says that).
- Why *this* work, not just any startup (shows they've thought about fit).
- They've shipped or completed things before (track record of follow-through).

**Red flag:** "I jump between interests" or "I'm still figuring out what I want" or a
vision that's generic.

### 7. Loyalty & Commitment (0–100)
**What it reads:** Stays and digs in through hard times. Not poached for a small raise.

**How it's scored:**
- Scenario s6: competitor offers +40% after we trained you (money vs loyalty)
- Scenario s7: company hits rough quarter (commitment under hardship)
- Driver pick: whether money is their most or least important driver

**What to look for in the written answer:**
- Do they talk about team or mission, or just pay and title?
- Have they stayed anywhere long-term, or jumped every year?
- Do they understand the trade-off (yes, you'll be poached, but why stay anyway)?

**Red flag:** "Salary is the main thing" or "Loyalty? I go where the best opportunity is."

## The Money Flag (Admin-Only)

**🟢 Green (Mission-leaning, 0–40):** Money-primary answers are rare. They rank money
low in the driver pick. They told a real "worked hard for no money" story. **Likely
loyal and mission-driven.**

**🟡 Amber (Mixed, 41–74):** Normal. They want fair pay AND growth. They picked money
somewhere in the middle. Typical startup builder.

**🔴 Red (Money-primary, 75–100):** Strong money signals. They picked "earn well soon"
as their top driver or ranked it high across scenarios. **Does not mean auto-reject** —
they might be brilliant — but means the loyalty trait score is the real check, and
**expect them to leave if poached aggressively**. The paid trial becomes critical here:
do they engage with the work, or are they half-hearted?

## Reading the Written Answers

The score **ranks**; the written answers **decide**.

### 1. First 72 Hours (mini work-sample)
**What to look for:** Do they understand the messiness? Can they be specific?

- Good: "Day 1: talk to the three disagreeing teams to understand what 'AI for cost
  reduction' means to each. Day 2: audit their data quality to find the limiting step.
  Day 3: sketch a quick 80% solution on the high-confidence data…"
- Bad: "I'd learn about the business and create a plan."

### 2. Learning Under Fire (behavioral STAR)
**What to look for:** Self-direction, speed, and a concrete outcome.

- Good: "I had to learn SQL in a week because our analyst was out. I grabbed a tutorial,
  built a test database from our live schema, and wrote my first query to pull cohort
  retention. It worked. We used it for the next 3 months of decisions."
- Bad: "I took an online course" (no urgency, no proof it worked).

### 3. Vision & Drive (3–5 years, why this work)
**What to look for:** Concrete ambition, not vague aspirations.

- Good: "I want to ship AI products that Indonesia actually uses. Right now most AI is
  built for English-first users. I'm here because TMK is building for real Southeast Asia
  problems. In 5 years I want to lead the product for our retail module."
- Bad: "I want to grow and learn and work with a good team" (every candidate says this).

### 4. Worked Hard for No Money (intrinsic motivation)
**What to look for:** Real struggle, real choice, real outcome.

- Good: "Built a side project to parse Indonesian banking APIs because I was frustrated
  with no open tools. Spent 6 months of nights and weekends. Never made money, but now
  two startups use it. I did it because the problem was real."
- Bad: "I volunteer" or "I did unpaid work for a friend's startup because I had to"
  (obligation, not choice).

## Decision Framework

**Strong hire (80+ score, green/amber money flag, strong written answers):**
- Invite to a paid trial. They're worth the test.

**Worth a trial (60–79, clear answers, minor red flags):**
- Weak in one trait (e.g., lower growth score) but strong elsewhere.
- Money flag is amber or red, but loyalty scenario picks show real commitment.
- Might just need the right team/context to shine.
- Paid trial: can they actually execute under real constraints?

**Likely no fit (40–59, inconsistent signals, vague written answers):**
- Mixed signals across traits; can't tell if they're strong but just writing poorly, or
  genuinely weak.
- Written answers are generic (everyone says "I want to learn").
- Check the specific weak trait. If it's Ownership or Tolerance, they'll struggle in a
  startup.

**Clear pass (0–39, red flags everywhere, money-primary with low loyalty):**
- Multiple traits are low.
- Written answers show they don't understand what they're signing up for.
- Money flag is red and loyalty scenario answers are self-interested.
- Move on.

## How to Use This in Practice

1. **Initial scan:** Sort by Builder %, skim money flags, glance at written answers for
   length/depth.
2. **Shortlist:** Flag 80+ scores or strong 60–79 with compelling written answers.
3. **Deep read:** Expand each shortlist row. Read all four written answers. Ask yourself:
   - Do they understand the messiness of early-stage?
   - Are they specific, or generic?
   - Which trait is strongest/weakest, and does that match what you need?
4. **Money/loyalty check:** If the money flag is red, has the loyalty score redeemed it
   (s6/s7 answers), or are they just chasing cash?
5. **Decide:** Strong candidates → paid trial. Borderline → re-read the written answers
   and trust your gut. Weak → pass.

---

## What the Score *Can't* Tell You

- **Fit for your team.** Someone might be a strong builder but wrong for the specific
  culture or problem you're solving.
- **Communication style.** A quiet, direct person might score high but not mesh with your
  collaborative, talkative team.
- **Reference checks.** The score is one lens; past managers' real stories are another.
- **The paid trial outcome.** A strong score isn't a guarantee; some people perform
  differently under real pressure.

**The score is a lens, not a verdict. Use it to sort and flag, then trust the written
answers and your intuition on the final call.**

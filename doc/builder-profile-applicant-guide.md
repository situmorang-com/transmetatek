# The Builder Profile: A Guide for Applicants

What we're actually looking for. What each trait means. How you can grow in these areas.

> This is not a job description. It's a realistic picture of what it takes to build at an
> early-stage startup — and how to know if it's right for you.

---

## Who is a startup builder?

A builder is someone who:
- **Keeps going** on hard, unglamorous work without being chased or celebrated
- **Learns fast** and independently — doesn't wait for a course to start moving
- **Owns problems** even when they're not formally your job
- **Stays** through the hard times instead of jumping for a small raise
- **Tolerates ambiguity, changing priorities, limited resources** — and doesn't crumble
- **Takes hard feedback** without ego, credits others, refuses to ship junk
- **Finishes what they start** and builds things that last, not just fast wins

If this sounds like you — or like something you want to become — read on.

## The 7 Builder Traits

We score you on these seven traits, 0–100 each. None of them are about how smart you are
or how many frameworks you know. They're about **how you actually work when things get hard**.

### 1. Drive & Motivation

**What it is:** Do you keep going on hard, unglamorous work without being chased? Do you
find motivation *inside* the work, not just from pay or status?

**How we read it:**
- A scenario: you're handed a boring, invisible task that actually matters to the company
  for a week. Do you grind it out, or find reasons to deprioritise it?
- Your written answer: tell us about a time you worked *hard* on something that had no
  money or reward in it — why did you actually keep going?

**How to strengthen it:**
- Do one project with no external reward — no pay, no public credit, just because it
  mattered. Write down why it mattered to you.
- Notice the unglamorous work in your current role — the refactoring no one will see, the
  data cleaning, the process improvement. Do it with the same care as your visible work.
- Ask yourself: if the money stopped tomorrow, would you still want to be here? If yes,
  that's drive. If no, that's information.

### 2. Growth & Learning

**What it is:** Do you learn fast when you *have to*, without waiting for someone to
teach you? Do you run toward things you can't do yet?

**How we read it:**
- A scenario: you're handed a tool you've never used with a real deadline, no training
  planned. Do you dig into the docs and try it, or wait for formal training?
- Your written answer: tell us about a time you *had* to learn something hard, fast —
  what did you actually do, and how did you prove it worked?

**How to strengthen it:**
- Pick something you can't do: a programming language, a framework, a design tool. Set a
  real deadline (one week, shipping something, not "someday learning").
- Document your learning process: what confused you, what you tried, what worked, what
  you'd do differently. That discipline matters as much as speed.
- **Ship something with it** — even tiny. "I learned Python" is fake learning. "I built a
  scraper in Python that solved a real problem" is real learning.

### 3. Ownership & Hard Work

**What it is:** Do you treat problems as yours even when they're not formally your job?
Do you find a way instead of saying "that's not my responsibility"?

**How we read it:**
- Scenarios: a vague goal and the owner's offline. Do you move forward with assumptions,
  or wait for clarity? An important problem nobody's assigned. Do you take it on, or
  leave it?
- Your written answer: tell us about a time you solved a problem that *wasn't* formally
  your job — why did you own it?

**How to strengthen it:**
- Look around your current role. What's broken that nobody's explicitly asked you to fix?
  Do it.
- When you see a problem, don't report it and wait. Come with a solution, even if it's
  scrappy.
- **Notice the difference:** "I told my boss and they fixed it" (not ownership). "I saw
  it was broken, I fixed it, then I told my boss what I'd done" (ownership).
- In your next team, never say "that's not my job." Instead, ask "who owns this, and how
  can I help?"

### 4. Startup Tolerance

**What it is:** Comfortable with ambiguity, changing priorities, limited resources.
Doesn't crumble under startup chaos. Sees constraints as problems to solve, not reasons
to give up.

**How we read it:**
- A scenario: the company's priorities shift mid-week. Your feature gets deprioritised.
  Do you feel the sting and move on, or get resentful and half-hearted?

**How to strengthen it:**
- **Volunteer for ambiguity:** ask to work on the one thing the team *isn't* sure about
  yet. See how you handle the fog.
- When priorities change, don't resist. Instead, ask "what do we know, and what's the
  smallest thing we can ship to learn faster?"
- Practice comfort with "I don't have all the info." Make a decision and move. You'll
  learn from the result.
- At your current job, notice: does a budget cut cause panic, or do you see it as a
  creative problem?

### 5. Low Ego, High Standard

**What it is:** You take hard feedback without ego. You credit others. You refuse to ship
junk. You see ambition as "ship the best," not "get credit for shipping."

**How we read it:**
- A scenario: a junior colleague flags a real flaw in your work *in front of everyone*.
  Do you defend, or thank them and fix it?
- Your written answers: do you credit collaborators, or center yourself? When you describe
  a failure, do you own it?

**How to strengthen it:**
- **Actively seek critical feedback.** "What did I miss?" is your opening question.
- In your writing (in this application, in your work), use we/us more than I. Notice it.
- Keep a project you're *proud of* — not for the credit, but because it's actually good.
  Don't let imposter syndrome stop you from having standards.
- Next time someone criticizes your work publicly, say "good catch, I missed that" and
  mean it.

### 6. Long-term Ambition

**What it is:** You finish what you start. You build things that last. You don't get
distracted by the shiny new thing every quarter. You have a real reason you're here *now*.

**How we read it:**
- A scenario: you're 4 months into a 12-month commitment. A flashier opportunity appears.
  Do you stay and finish, or jump?
- Your written answer: where do you want to be in 3–5 years, and *why is this work the
  right step*? (Not "I want to grow and learn." Why *here*?)

**How to strengthen it:**
- **Write down your real ambition** — where you want to be in 5 years, what you want to
  build. Be specific: not "a successful startup," but "I want to ship AI products for
  Indonesia."
- Look at your track record. Have you finished things? Have you stuck with anything for
  more than a year? (Yes = good. No = learn this.)
- When you apply, be *specific* about why this role, this team, this problem. "I want to
  grow" applies to 10,000 jobs. "I want to build for Indonesia because [reason]" applies
  to maybe one.
- Notice: do you finish things or abandon them? Do you ship and then move on, or do you
  stay and improve it? Builders tend to stay.

### 7. Loyalty & Commitment

**What it is:** You stay through hard times instead of jumping for a small raise or easy
alternative. You're invested in the people and the mission, not just the paycheck.

**How we read it:**
- Scenarios: a competitor offers you 40% more *after we've trained you*. Do you use it
  as leverage, or stay because you believe in what we're building?
- When the company hits rough water, do you dig in or look for the exit?
- What drives you most: money, growth, mission, team, or a mix?

**How to strengthen it:**
- **Understand your own drivers.** Is money your top priority? (Not wrong, but be honest.)
  Or mission? Growth? Team? Different people have different drivers, and that's OK — but
  know yours.
- If you've left jobs, why? "Better pay" is honest. "Better team and mission" is
  different. "My boss was jerks and the work didn't matter" is real. Notice the pattern.
- In your next role, ask yourself: **what would make me stay if things got hard?** If the
  answer is only "more money," you're not a fit for startup life.
- Talk to people who've been at startups through rough patches. Ask them why they stayed.

## How the Assessment Works

You don't see the score during the form — that's on purpose. We want you to answer *straight*,
not try to optimize for what looks good.

**The form has three parts:**

1. **What drives you (forced choice).** Pick one thing that matters *most* and one that
   matters *least* from: build something lasting, get better fast, own big things, earn
   well soon, respected title, comfort & stability. You can't claim everything matters —
   that forces honesty.

2. **Real scenarios (12 situations).** For each, pick the best and worst response from four
   options. None of them are obvious "good" answers — they're all plausible. We're not
   looking for the "right" answer; we're looking at what you actually value.

3. **Proof (your words).** Write short answers about real things you've done: first 72
   hours on a messy situation, a time you learned something hard and fast, where you're
   headed in 3–5 years, and a time you worked hard when there was little reward. **Be
   specific. Specifics are how we know it's real.**

We score you on the 7 traits, but **the traits are just a lens.** Your written answers are the
real decision — we read them by hand, and they're what tells us who you actually are.

## If You Don't Get Selected

It's not because you're not good. It might mean:
- **You're stronger in a different profile** — maybe you're brilliant and want a clear
  structure, growth plan, and predictable work. That's not a builder profile, and that's
  honest.
- **You're not convinced yet.** Some people discover they *want* startup life, but aren't
  there yet. That's OK — come back when you are.
- **Different team chemistry.** Your traits might be strong, but the fit with our team or
  problem isn't there. We're looking for both.

---

## If You Get Selected

You'll be invited to a **paid trial project** before a final offer. That's not a test to
fail — it's real work, real pay, real chance to see if you like how we operate before
either of us commits. Some people score high but realize startup life isn't for them. Some
people score lower but excel in the real thing. The trial is where both of you learn.

## The Honest Pitch

If you're someone who:
- Loves finishing things, not just starting them
- Wants to work on something that matters (more than just a paycheck)
- Can handle ambiguity and see it as a creative problem, not a blocker
- Learns fast and independently
- Takes hard feedback without getting defensive
- Has a real reason you want to build *now*

Then the startup builder life might be exactly right for you. And we want to find you.

If you're still figuring out whether this is you, the assessment is designed to help *you*
understand yourself better — not to trick you. Answer straight. That's all we ask.
